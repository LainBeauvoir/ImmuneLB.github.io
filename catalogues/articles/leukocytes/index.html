White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)

White blood cells, also known as leukocytes, are immune system cells responsible for detecting, responding to, and eliminating pathogens and abnormal cells. They circulate through blood and tissues, coordinating both innate and adaptive immune responses.

White blood cells are a diverse group of cells that form the cellular foundation of the immune system. Unlike red blood cells, leukocytes are actively involved in identifying threats, communicating immune signals, and executing targeted responses against foreign and harmful entities.

Rather than functioning as a single uniform population, white blood cells consist of multiple specialized cell types, each adapted to distinct roles such as pathogen recognition, antibody production, inflammation, and immune memory.

White blood cells are broadly classified based on their structure, function, and role within the immune response. These classifications help distinguish between immediate, non-specific defenses and highly targeted adaptive mechanisms.

Category: Body Cells

System: Immune System

Primary Function: Defense and immune coordination

Origin: Bone marrow

Circulation: Blood, lymphatic system, tissues

Also Known As: Leukocytes

Neutrophils

Neutrophils are the most abundant type of granulocytes and make up 40% to 70% of all white blood cells in humans. They form an essential part of the innate immune system. Neutrophils are formed from stem cells in the bone marrow and are short-lived and highly motile, as they can enter parts of tissue where other cells or molecules cannot.

They are "first responders" to inflammation, particularly as a result of bacterial infection, environmental exposure, and some cancers. They move through the blood vessels, then through interstitial tissue, following chemical signals such as Interleukin-8 in a process called chemotaxis.

Type: Granulocyte

Life Span: 5–90 hours

Target: Bacteria & Fungi

Lymphocytes

Lymphocytes are one of the main types of immune cells. They are divided into B cells, T cells, and natural killer (NK) cells. B cells produce antibodies that are used to attack invading bacteria, viruses, and toxins. T cells destroy the body's own cells that have itself been taken over by viruses or become cancerous.

Unlike the innate response of neutrophils, lymphocytes are the backbone of the adaptive immune system. They "remember" previous invaders, allowing the body to mount a much faster and stronger attack if the same pathogen returns years later.

Type: Agranulocyte

Life Span: Weeks to Years

Target: Viruses & Tumors

Monocytes

Monocytes are the largest type of leukocyte and can differentiate into macrophages and myeloid lineage dendritic cells. As part of the vertebrate innate immune system, monocytes also influence the process of adaptive immunity. They are produced by the bone marrow from precursors called monoblasts.

Monocytes serve three main functions: phagocytosis, antigen presentation, and cytokine production. Phagocytosis is the process of uptake of microbes and particles followed by their digestion and destruction. They act as "scouts" that alert the rest of the immune system to a breach.

Type: Agranulocyte

Life Span: 1–3 days (in blood)

Target: Pathogen debris

Eosinophils

Eosinophils, or eosinocytes, are a variety of white blood cells and one of the immune system components responsible for combating multicellular parasites and certain infections in vertebrates. Along with mast cells and basophils, they also control mechanisms associated with allergy and asthma.

They are granulocytes that develop during hematopoiesis in the bone marrow before migrating into blood, after which they are terminally differentiated and do not multiply. Their most distinctive feature is the presence of large granules that stain bright red/orange with eosin dye.

Type: Granulocyte

Life Span: 8–12 days

Target: Parasites & Allergens

Basophils

Basophils are the least common type of granulocyte, representing about 0.5% to 1% of circulating white blood cells. However, they are the largest type of granulocyte. They are responsible for inflammatory reactions during immune response, as well as in the formation of acute and chronic allergic diseases.

Basophils contain large cytoplasmic granules which obscure the cell nucleus under the microscope when stained. These granules are rich in histamine and heparin, which help increase blood flow to a site of infection and prevent blood from clotting too quickly.

Type: Granulocyte

Life Span: Hours to Days

Target: Inflammatory triggers
